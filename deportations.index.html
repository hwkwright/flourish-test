Over four days in March 1949, more than 90,000 Estonians, Latvians and Lithuanians were forcibly deported to Russia from their homelands by the USSR

Men, women and children were packed into cattle cars and transported by rail to Siberia

The acts which took place between March 25 and 29 - four years after the Second World War ended

The majority of those sent into exile were women and children

Many never returned.

The USSR occupied Estonia, Latvia and Lithuania for 50 years, until 1991, and the topic was suppressed.

Now these countries have regained their independence, the deportations are marked every March.

In Estonia, candles are it on Freedom Sqaure in Tallinn, the capital.

Which burn all night.

They commemorate those who never returned.

Their names are also projected onto walls around the sqaure.

And politicians and foreign diplomats also lay memorial wreaths at the monument on Tallinn's seafront.

Where the names of those who died at the hands of the USSR are engraved on the walls.

Estonians also leave flowers and candles here in June, to commemorate the first wave of deportations in 1940.
